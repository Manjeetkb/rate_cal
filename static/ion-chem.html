Theory of Ion-molecule Reactions

Mass spectrometry has provided great deal of information of ion-molecule reactions including extensive thermodynamic information about ions and molecules which include standard enthalpy of ion formation, electron affinities, ionization energies and proton affinities of neutral molecules. Ion-molecule reactions are among the fastest chemical reactions because the electrostatic interaction between ionic charge and polar molecule draws them (ion and molecule) together at long range. The interaction energy as a result is large enough to overcome intrinsic energy barrier to chemical change. As a consequences, these reactions often proceed through zero or very small activation energy at room temperature. The reaction rate coefficients will be determined by collision rate and found to be of the order of ~1*10-9 cm3 mol-1 s-1.

Rate Coefficients in Ion-molecule Collision

The rate coefficients of ion-molecule reactions that proceed without activation energy can not be obtained by Arrhenius equation. Although, rate coefficients of such exothermic reactions is given by the capture collision cross-section. Several theories are available that allow calculation of the capture or collision rate coefficient for an ion-molecule collision. According to the most popular theory, the average dipole orientation (ADO) theory, the magnitude of the rates is determined by the physical properties of the reactants: the polarizability and permanent dipole of the reacting molecule.
